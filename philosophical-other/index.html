The Other and Its Discontents

The philosophical threads behind one Malayalam couplet

The line  ·  its gloss

ആരാന്റെ സെമിനാറിൽ വാ, എന്റെ 'വിളമ്പൽ' കാണാൻ

(An arch reverse-'othering' for utilitarian appropriation)

The couplet's parenthetical leans on a loaded word — 'othering' — and disarms it on purpose. To see what the disarming costs and what it earns, it helps to lay out the apparatus the word carries. What follows is a short map of the figures it touches, in rough order of descent: from the neutral metaphysical Other down to the pejorative social 'othering', and back up again to the one thinker who turns the whole thing on its head.

A note on confidence: the attributions, works, and dates below are established scholarship. The final section — how this bears on the couplet — is interpretive, and marked as such.

The two faces of “the Other”

The single biggest source of confusion here is that “the Other” names two different things that share a word.

Alterity (the neutral sense). The Other as another consciousness — the bare fact that there are minds not my own, given to me differently from how I am given to myself. A structural, descriptive problem: how do I know there are other subjects at all?

'Othering' (the pejorative sense). A verb — the operation by which a dominant group constitutes someone as alien, lesser, or marginal in order to secure its own centrality. Normative and critical: 'othering' is something done to people, and it is bad.

Almost every dispute in this territory is really a dispute about which face is in play. The couplet exploits exactly this fork: it invokes “the other” (the innocent sense — somebody else's seminar) while naming the loaded sense only to refuse it.

I

The root: recognitionG. W. F. Hegel

The whole tradition grows from Hegel's Phenomenology of Spirit (1807), and specifically the master–slave (lord–bondsman) dialectic. Hegel's claim is radical: self-consciousness is not self-sufficient. I become a self only by being recognised (Anerkennung) by another self-consciousness. The Other is therefore constitutive of me, not an optional extra. The dialectic stages a struggle in which one consciousness dominates and the other serves — and, in the famous twist, it is the bondsman, through labour on the world, who develops the richer selfhood.

Everything downstream — Sartre's conflict, de Beauvoir's subordinated woman, the colonial relation — is a variation on this single insight: the self is built through the Other, and that building can go wrong.

II

The problem statedEdmund Husserl

Husserl (Cartesian Meditations, Fifth Meditation, 1931) gives the phenomenological statement of the neutral sense. How is the Other given to me? I never have direct access to another's inner life; I encounter a body “over there” resembling mine, and through what Husserl calls analogical appresentation I grasp it as housing an alter ego — another “I.” The Other is constituted in my experience, yet given precisely as that which exceeds it. This is the dignified, difficult version of the problem — and it sets the table for the revolt against it.

III

Solipsism and the analytic “other minds”Bertrand Russell & the analytic tradition

Analytic philosophy meets the same neutral problem under a different name: the Problem of Other Minds. Solipsism is its limiting, sceptical case — the position that only my own mind can be known to exist (or, stronger, only my mind exists at all). Where the phenomenologists describe how the Other is given, the analytic question is blunter: can the existence of other minds be proved — by analogy with my own case, by inference to the best explanation, by Wittgenstein's appeal to public criteria?

Russell handled solipsism with characteristic lightness. In Human Knowledge: Its Scope and Limits (1948) he relays a letter from the logician Christine Ladd-Franklin, who wryly declared herself a solipsist and professed surprise that there weren't more of them — to which Russell, deadpan, replied that coming from a logician her surprise surprised him. She was plainly in on the joke: a logician of her calibre would have seen the self-undermining point at once, that the moment a solipsist wishes for company — or writes a letter expecting a reader — the game is already conceded. Solipsism, on Russell's reckoning, is hard to refute in logic and impossible to believe in life.

The comedy has a serious edge: a solipsist hoping to recruit fellow solipsists is the exact inverse of the figure in our couplet — the performer who, far from doubting his audience, depends on it utterly while treating it as mere scenery.

IV

The Look, and “Hell is other people”Jean-Paul Sartre

Sartre supplies the darkest version of the neutral sense, in Being and Nothingness (1943). His key figure is the Look (le regard). As a pure subject I am freedom, the centre of my own world. But when the Other looks at me, I become an object in their world — fixed, classified, judged, my freedom stolen. The other person drains my world towards themselves; I am the man caught mid-keyhole, seen. This is 'othering' as objectification, described from the inside.

The famous line — “Hell is other people” (“L'enfer, c'est les autres”) — closes his 1944 play Huis Clos (No Exit): three dead characters locked in a room, tormenting one another for eternity, with no torturer but each other.

The crucial nuance is that Sartre thought the line badly misread as plain misanthropy. He insisted he did not mean that other people are hell. He meant that we know ourselves through others' judgements: the Other holds a mirror we cannot escape. If our relations are twisted or corrupt, that mirror shows a hellish self, and we live imprisoned in others' verdicts. Hell is not other people as such — it is bad faith conducted through other people. (That machinery, precisely, is what the satire runs on: the grandstander who exists only in the host's approving gaze.)

V

Not Sartre: the stranger and solidarityAlbert Camus

It is worth settling a common mix-up: the “hell” line is Sartre's, not Camus's — and the contrast is instructive, because Camus's relation to the Other runs the opposite way. Camus has a stranger, but it is the estranged self: Meursault in L'Étranger (1942), condemned less for murder than for being illegible to others. And Camus has a bond Sartre lacks: in The Rebel (1951), rebellion against the absurd issues in solidarity — the move from “I rebel” to “we exist.” Where Sartre's Other is a thief of my freedom, Camus's revolt discovers the Other as a comrade in a shared condition. The absurd and the outsider are Camus; the Look and “hell is other people” are Sartre.

VI

The Other turned upwardEmmanuel Levinas

Levinas (Totality and Infinity, 1961; Otherwise than Being, 1974) performs the reversal that makes the rest of this note matter. The entire Western tradition — Husserl and Sartre included — has tried, he argues, to reduce the Other to the Same: to comprehend, categorise, totalise the Other within my own horizon. That reduction is the original violence.

Against it he sets the face (le visage). The face is not a thing I perceive and file; it commands me. It issues an infinite ethical demand — answer for me — before I have chosen anything. From this comes his signature claim: ethics is first philosophy. My responsibility for the Other precedes my freedom and even my being. This is also the deepest refutation of solipsism, but not the analytic one: where the analytic tradition tries to argue its way out, Levinas says the face ruptures the solipsistic totality before any argument can begin.

The terms that matter here are welcome and hospitality: the genuinely positive relation to the Other is to receive them, to make room. Levinas is the philosopher of the Other as the one I am for — which is exactly the relation the satire's target parodies.

VII

Woman as the OtherSimone de Beauvoir

The Second Sex (1949) is where the neutral Other becomes the social operation of 'othering'. Taking Hegel's recognition-struggle and Sartre's self/other (she was his lifelong interlocutor), de Beauvoir argues that in patriarchal culture man sets himself up as the Subject, the Absolute, the norm — and defines woman as the Other, the inessential, defined only relative to him. Man is the “One”; woman is what he is not.

This is the decisive move for the modern usage: 'othering' as a one-directional, asymmetrical subordination, not a mutual struggle for recognition. (Her separate thesis — that one becomes rather than is born a woman — supplies the constructivist engine: the Other is made, not found.) De Beauvoir is the bridge from German idealism to the critical-theoretical sense of the term the couplet plays with.

VIII

'Othering' becomes a verbEdward Said & Gayatri Spivak

The pejorative sense hardens into a technical instrument in postcolonial theory. Said's Orientalism (1978) shows how the West produced “the Orient” as its exotic, irrational Other — a discursive construction that served, and justified, domination. Spivak's Can the Subaltern Speak? (1988) asks whether the colonised Other can be heard at all, or only ever spoken for. Here “to other” settles fully into the lexicon as a transitive verb of power.

By the time the word reaches everyday critical usage, it carries all of this: a near-automatic charge of domination, asymmetry, and bad conscience. That is the freight the parenthetical knows it is handling.

Back to the couplet

How this bears on the line

Interpretive — a reading, not a claim about authorial intent.

  1. The term-level move. The gloss announces full awareness that 'othering' is always pejorative (de Beauvoir → Said → Spivak), then deliberately refuses the operation: it uses the bare word “other” in its innocent sense — somebody else's seminar — while flagging, with a wink, that it is not doing the loaded one. It utilises the other without 'othering' the other. “Arch” is the right register because the act is neither careless nor wicked but knowing — a scrupulous, playful disarming of a loaded term for satirical use. That is the reverse-'othering': the operation run backwards, its charge defused.
  2. The Levinasian undertow. The scene parodies Levinasian hospitality. വിളമ്പൽ — serving, dishing out — is the host's gesture. The hypocrite is a guest in ആരാന്റെ seminar (hospitality received) and repays it by usurping the host's role, converting the host's room into a stage for the self. Levinas's positive Other-relation is welcome; the target's move is its inversion — welcome accepted and annexed.
  3. The Sartrean engine. The grandstander exists only in the approving Look of host and audience; his lofty performance is bad faith conducted through others' gaze — Sartre's “hell,” exactly. The satire works because it ventriloquises that gaze-dependence and lets it expose itself.

So a two-line Malayalam jab quietly threads recognition (Hegel), the objectifying Look (Sartre), the sceptical limit of the solitary mind (Russell's solipsist), the social manufacture of the Other (de Beauvoir), the critical verb (Said and Spivak), and the hospitality it parodies (Levinas) — while the gloss steps outside all of it to wink at the reader.
